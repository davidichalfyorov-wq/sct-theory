\begin{abstract}
We derive the Parameterized Post-Newtonian (PPN) parameters of Spectral
Causal Theory (SCT) at three levels of approximation.
\textbf{Level~1} (exact nonlocal) formulates the gravitational potentials
$\Phi(r)$ and $\Psi(r)$ as Fourier--Bessel integrals over the full SCT
propagator denominators $\PiTT(z)$ and $\Pis(z,\xi)$, and identifies the
structural obstructions to naive numerical evaluation (nonzero UV asymptote,
real-axis pole, prescription mismatch).
\textbf{Level~2} (local Yukawa) replaces the propagator denominators by
their leading pole approximants, yielding analytic Yukawa potentials with
two effective masses $m_2 = \Lambda\sqrt{60/13}$ and
$m_0 = \Lambda/\!\sqrt{6(\xi-1/6)^2}$, from which the linear PPN parameter
$\gamma(r)$ and lower bounds on $\Lambda$ are extracted.
\textbf{Level~3} (nonlinear PPN) identifies the remaining parameters
($\beta$, $\alpha_i$, $\zeta_i$, $\xi_\mathrm{PPN}$) as requiring the
$\order(h^2)$ field equations from NT-4b, and marks them as not yet derived.

Within the Level~2 approximation, the Cassini bound gives
$\Lambda \gtrsim 6.3 \times 10^{-18}\,\mathrm{eV}$, the MESSENGER bound
gives $\Lambda \gtrsim 6.3 \times 10^{-18}\,\mathrm{eV}$, and the
Eot-Wash torsion-balance bound gives
$\Lambda \gtrsim 2.4 \times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{eV}$.  The MICROSCOPE and
Lunar Laser Ranging bounds require $\beta$ and are deferred.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Purpose and Structure}

The purpose of PPN-1 is to connect the verified spectral form factors and
the NT-4a propagator with observable weak-field quantities.  The derivation
proceeds in three nested levels of approximation:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Level \arabic*}:, leftmargin=4em]
  \item \textbf{Exact nonlocal.}  The gravitational potentials are written
    as Fourier--Bessel integrals involving the full propagator denominators.
    This is mathematically exact (within the linearized static sector) but
    not yet numerically implemented in a reliable way due to a real-axis
    pole and a nonzero UV asymptote in the momentum-space kernels.

  \item \textbf{Local Yukawa.}  The propagator denominators are replaced by
    their leading simple-pole approximants, reducing the potentials to
    Stelle-like Yukawa form.  This is the operative approximation for all
    solar-system phenomenology and is fully verified.

  \item \textbf{Nonlinear PPN.}  The $\order(h^2)$ terms in $g_{00}$, the
    $g_{0i}$ gravitomagnetic sector, and the nonlinear superposition
    structure are needed for the remaining PPN parameters ($\beta$,
    $\alpha_i$, $\zeta_i$, $\xi_\mathrm{PPN}$).  These require the
    nonlinear field equations from NT-4b and are not derived here.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The following quantities are \emph{not} claimed in this document:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*)]
  \item a full ten-parameter PPN completion,
  \item a derivation of $\beta$,
  \item a proof that the preferred-frame or conservation parameters vanish,
  \item a reliable numerical evaluation of the exact nonlocal Fourier
    integrals at all radii.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Metric and Sign Conventions}

We use the Lorentzian weak-field form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  g_{00} = -1 + 2\Phi(r), \qquad
  g_{ij} = \bigl(1 + 2\Psi(r)\bigr)\delta_{ij},
\end{equation}
with signature $(-,+,+,+)$.  In the static limit, the physical potential
energy of a non-relativistic test body is
\begin{equation}
  V(r) = -m_{\mathrm{test}}\,\Phi(r).
\end{equation}
The Newtonian limit corresponds to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:newton}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{N}}(r) = \Psi_{\mathrm{N}}(r) = \frac{GM}{r},
  \qquad
  V_{\mathrm{N}}(r) = -\frac{GMm_{\mathrm{test}}}{r}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Dimensional Conventions}

Natural units $c=\hbar=1$ are used throughout.  For phenomenology we
restore
\begin{equation}
  \hbar c = 1.9732705 \times 10^{-7}\,\mathrm{eV\cdot m},
  \qquad
  1\,\mathrm{m} \simeq 5.06773 \times 10^{6}\,\mathrm{eV}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Key length scales:
\begin{align}
  1\,\mathrm{AU}
    &\simeq 1.496 \times 10^{11}\,\mathrm{m}
    \simeq 7.58 \times 10^{17}\,\mathrm{eV}^{-1}, \\
  38.6\,\mu\mathrm{m}
    &\simeq 1.96 \times 10^{2}\,\mathrm{eV}^{-1}.
\end{align}

\section{Level 1: Exact Linear Static Potentials}
\label{sec:level1}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{From Linearized Field Equations to Fourier Representation}
\label{sec:fourier-derivation}

Consider a static, spherically symmetric point source:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source}
  T_{00} = M\,\delta^{(3)}(\vec{x}),
  \qquad
  T_{0i} = T_{ij} = 0.
\end{equation}
The linearized SCT field equations (NT-4a) in Fourier space, after
decomposition into spin-2 (transverse-traceless) and spin-0 (scalar trace)
sectors via Barnes--Rivers projectors $P^{(2)}$ and $P^{(0\text{-}s)}$,
take the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin-fourier}
  \PiTT(k^2/\Lambda^2)\,\tilde{h}^{\mathrm{TT}}_{\mu\nu}(k)
  = -\frac{16\pi G}{k^2}\,P^{(2)}_{\mu\nu,\rho\sigma}\,\tilde{T}^{\rho\sigma}(k),
\end{equation}
and similarly for the scalar sector with $\Pis$ and $P^{(0\text{-}s)}$.
The metric perturbation in position space is obtained by inverse Fourier
transform.

For the static, isotropic metric \eqref{eq:metric}, the two potentials
$\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are extracted by projecting onto $g_{00}$ and
$g_{ij} \propto \delta_{ij}$ components.  The spin-2 mode contributes to
both $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ with definite relative weights (the van~Dam--Veltman
factor of $4/3$ for spin-2 vs.\ spin-0 in the temporal potential), while
the spin-0 mode contributes with $-1/3$ to $\Phi$ and $+1/3$ to $\Psi$.
This yields the Newton kernels
\begin{equation}\label{eq:KPhi}
  \KPhi(z) = \frac{4}{3\,\PiTT(z)} - \frac{1}{3\,\Pis(z,\xi)},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:KPsi}
  \KPsi(z) = \frac{2}{3\,\PiTT(z)} + \frac{1}{3\,\Pis(z,\xi)},
\end{equation}
where $z = k^2/\Lambda^2$.  At $z = 0$:
$\KPhi(0) = 4/3 - 1/3 = 1$ and $\KPsi(0) = 2/3 + 1/3 = 1$, recovering
the Newtonian limit $\Phi = \Psi = GM/r$.

\subsection{Angular Averaging: From 3D Fourier to Fourier--Bessel}
\label{sec:angular-averaging}

The position-space potential is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(r) = \frac{GM}{(2\pi)^3}
  \int \dd^3k\, \frac{e^{i\vec{k}\cdot\vec{r}}}{k^2}\,\KPhi(k^2/\Lambda^2).
\end{equation}
In spherical coordinates $(k, \theta, \varphi)$ with $\vec{k}\cdot\vec{r}
= kr\cos\theta$, the angular integral gives
\begin{equation}
  \int_0^{2\pi}\!\dd\varphi \int_0^\pi\!\dd\theta\,\sin\theta\,
  e^{ikr\cos\theta}
  = 2\pi \cdot \frac{2\sin(kr)}{kr}
  = \frac{4\pi\sin(kr)}{kr}.
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(r) = \frac{GM}{(2\pi)^3}\int_0^\infty\!
  4\pi k^2\dd k\,\frac{1}{k^2}\,\frac{4\pi\sin(kr)}{kr}\,
  \KPhi(k^2/\Lambda^2)
  = \frac{GM}{r}\,\frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty
  \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,\KPhi(k^2/\Lambda^2)\,\dd k.
\end{equation}
The factor $GM/r$ has been separated using the standard Dirichlet integral
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dirichlet}
  \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,\dd k = 1,
  \qquad r > 0.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Exact linear static potentials]
\label{prop:exact-potentials}
For the point source \eqref{eq:source}, the linearized SCT potentials are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi-exact}
  \frac{\Phi(r)}{\Phi_{\mathrm{N}}(r)}
  = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,
  \KPhi(k^2/\Lambda^2)\,\dd k,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Psi-exact}
  \frac{\Psi(r)}{\Psi_{\mathrm{N}}(r)}
  = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,
  \KPsi(k^2/\Lambda^2)\,\dd k.
\end{equation}
These are exact within the linearized static sector.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{UV Behavior of the Newton Kernels}
\label{sec:uv-kernels}

Unlike the Stelle (local higher-derivative) case, where $\PiTT(z) = 1 + c_2 z$
grows linearly and hence $\KPhi(z) \to 0$ as $z \to \infty$, the SCT
propagator denominators involve the entire form factor $\Fhat_1(z)$ which
has a nontrivial large-$z$ asymptotic.  Specifically, the NT-1b Phase~3
result gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:uv-PiTT}
  \PiTT(z) \xrightarrow{z\to\infty}
  1 + c_2\,z\,\Fhat_1(z)
  \;\to\;
  c_2\,\bigl(-\tfrac{89}{12}\bigr)\cdot\frac{1}{\alpha_C}
  = -\frac{89}{12}\cdot\frac{F_1(\infty)}{F_1(0)}\cdot c_2\,z\to\cdots
\end{equation}
Numerically, $\PiTT(z)$ saturates to a finite negative value:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PiTT-inf}
  \PiTT(z \to \infty) \approx -13.83.
\end{equation}
This means the Newton kernel has a nonzero UV asymptote:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:KPhi-inf}
  \KPhi(z \to \infty)
  = \frac{4}{3 \cdot (-13.83)} - \frac{1}{3 \cdot \Pis(\infty)}
  \approx -0.136.
\end{equation}
Consequently, the correction integral
\[
  \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\bigl[\KPhi(z) - 1\bigr]\dd k
\]
converges only conditionally through the oscillatory $\sin(kr)$ factor,
not absolutely.  This is a fundamental difference from Stelle gravity
where $\KPhi - 1 \to -1$ at $z \to \infty$, making the integral
absolutely convergent after standard Yukawa decomposition.

\subsection{The TT Pole on the Real Axis}
\label{sec:tt-pole}

The propagator denominator $\PiTT(z)$ has a zero on the positive real
axis at
\begin{equation}\label{eq:z0}
  z_0 \approx 2.41484\ldots,
  \qquad
  \PiTT(z_0) = 0.
\end{equation}
This zero produces a simple pole in $1/\PiTT(z)$, and hence in $\KPhi(z)$
and $\KPsi(z)$, at $z = z_0$.  In momentum space, this corresponds to a
pole at $k = k_0 \coloneqq \Lambda\sqrt{z_0}$ on the real integration axis
of the Fourier--Bessel integrals \eqref{eq:Phi-exact}--\eqref{eq:Psi-exact}.

The residue of the pole in $1/\PiTT(z)$ at $z = z_0$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:residue}
  R_{\mathrm{pole}} = \frac{1}{\PiTT'(z_0)}
  \approx -1.191,
\end{equation}
where $\PiTT'(z_0) \approx -0.8398$.

\begin{remark}[Comparison with Stelle gravity]
In Stelle's local theory, $\PiTT(z) = 1 + c_2 z$ has its zero at
$z_{\mathrm{Stelle}} = -1/c_2 = -60/13 \approx -4.615$, which lies on the
\emph{negative} real axis. Since $z = k^2/\Lambda^2 \geq 0$ on the
integration domain, the Stelle pole is at imaginary $k$ and produces a
standard Yukawa term $e^{-m_2 r}$ without entering the integration contour.
In SCT, the zero at $z_0 \approx 2.41 > 0$ lies on the integration domain,
creating a qualitatively different analytic structure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Prescription Issue for the Pole Contribution}
\label{sec:prescription}

The pole at $k = k_0$ on the real integration axis requires a prescription.
Two natural choices exist:

\paragraph{Principal value (PV).}
Subtracting the pole on the real axis and adding back the PV integral
analytically.  The Gradshteyn--Ryzhik identity (3.723.2) for the
real-axis pole gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pv}
  \frac{2}{\pi}\,\mathrm{P.V.}\!\int_0^\infty
  \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,\frac{\Lambda^2}{k^2 - k_0^2}\,\dd k
  = \frac{1 - \cos(k_0 r)}{k_0^2/\Lambda^2},
\end{equation}
producing an \emph{oscillatory} contribution $\propto \cos(k_0 r)$.

\paragraph{Euclidean prescription.}
The spectral action is defined via the Euclidean path integral, where
$k^2 \to -k_E^2$, and the pole in $1/\PiTT$ contributes as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:euclidean}
  \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty
  \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,\frac{k_0^2}{k^2 + k_0^2}\,\dd k
  = \frac{1 - e^{-k_0 r}}{1},
\end{equation}
producing a \emph{Yukawa-damped} contribution $\propto e^{-k_0 r}$.

\begin{remark}[Prescription mismatch]
\label{rem:mismatch}
If the regularized correction integral (with the pole subtracted on the
real $k$-axis) is combined with the Euclidean pole contribution, the two
pieces are \emph{inconsistent}: the subtracted PV integral removes the
cosine behavior while the addback introduces exponential damping, creating
a mismatch of magnitude
\begin{equation}
  \Delta = \frac{4}{3}\,\frac{R_{\mathrm{pole}}}{z_0}\,
  \bigl[2 - e^{-k_0 r} - \cos(k_0 r)\bigr].
\end{equation}
A numerically correct treatment requires either (a) direct
Euclidean-space integration, (b) Levin-type oscillatory quadrature with
analytic continuation, or (c) contour deformation in the complex
$k$-plane.  These methods are deferred to a dedicated numerical study.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Current Status of Level 1}

The Level~1 formulation is mathematically exact and well-defined.
However, the numerical implementation of the Fourier--Bessel integrals
is not yet reliable due to the real-axis pole (\cref{sec:tt-pole}) and
the nonzero UV asymptote (\cref{sec:uv-kernels}).  The functions
\texttt{phi\_exact}, \texttt{psi\_exact}, \texttt{gamma\_exact} in
\texttt{ppn1\_parameters.py} document the mathematical structure but are
marked as \textsc{placeholder}.  For all quantitative phenomenology,
Level~2 applies.

\begin{remark}[Validity of Level 2 for solar-system tests]
For any viable $\Lambda$ (i.e., $\Lambda$ satisfying the experimental
bounds below), we have $r_\mathrm{AU}\cdot m_2 \gg 1$.  In this regime,
the Yukawa corrections are exponentially suppressed, and the difference
between the exact nonlocal potentials and the local Yukawa approximation
is smaller than the current experimental precision.  Level~2 is therefore
the correct approximation for all solar-system observables at present.
\end{remark}

\section{Level 2: Local Yukawa Approximation}
\label{sec:level2}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{Small-\texorpdfstring{$z$}{z} Expansion and Effective Masses}
\label{sec:masses}

For $k^2 \ll \Lambda^2$ (i.e., $z \ll 1$), the normalized form factors
satisfy $\Fhat_i(z) = 1 + \order(z)$, so
\begin{equation}
  \PiTT(z) = 1 + c_2\,z + \order(z^2),
  \qquad
  \Pis(z,\xi) = 1 + (3c_1+c_2)\,z + \order(z^2).
\end{equation}
The denominators have the same form as Stelle gravity with effective masses
\begin{equation}\label{eq:m2}
  m_2^2 = \frac{\Lambda^2}{c_2}
  = \frac{60}{13}\,\Lambda^2,
  \qquad
  m_2 = \Lambda\sqrt{\frac{60}{13}} \approx 2.1483\,\Lambda,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:m0}
  m_0^2 = \frac{\Lambda^2}{3c_1+c_2}
  = \frac{\Lambda^2}{6(\xi-1/6)^2},
  \qquad
  m_0(\xi=0) = \Lambda\sqrt{6} \approx 2.4495\,\Lambda.
\end{equation}
At exact conformal coupling $\xi = 1/6$, the scalar mode decouples and
$m_0 = \infty$.

\subsection{Local Yukawa Potentials via Fourier--Bessel Integral}
\label{sec:yukawa-potentials}

In the local approximation, the propagator denominators become simple
polynomials: $\PiTT(z) \approx 1 + c_2 z$ and
$\Pis(z,\xi) \approx 1 + (3c_1+c_2)z$.  The Newton kernels decompose
into partial fractions:
\begin{equation}
  \KPhi(z) = \frac{4}{3}\,\frac{1}{1+c_2 z} - \frac{1}{3}\,\frac{1}{1+(3c_1+c_2)z}
  = 1 + \frac{4}{3}\,\frac{m_2^2}{k^2+m_2^2}\cdot(-1)
  - \frac{1}{3}\,\frac{m_0^2}{k^2+m_0^2}\cdot(-1) + \cdots
\end{equation}
More precisely, using $1/(1+c_2 z) = m_2^2/(k^2+m_2^2)$, we have
\begin{equation}
  \KPhi^{\mathrm{loc}}(z)
  = \frac{4}{3}\,\frac{m_2^2}{k^2+m_2^2}
  - \frac{1}{3}\,\frac{m_0^2}{k^2+m_0^2}.
\end{equation}
The Fourier--Bessel identity (Gradshteyn--Ryzhik 3.723.2) states
\begin{equation}\label{eq:GR-identity}
  \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(kr)}{k}\,\frac{m^2}{k^2+m^2}\,\dd k
  = 1 - e^{-mr},
  \qquad r > 0,\; m > 0.
\end{equation}
Applying this to each term:

\begin{proposition}[Local Yukawa potentials]
\label{prop:local-potentials}
In the local Yukawa approximation, for $\xi \neq 1/6$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi-local}
  \boxed{
  \frac{\Phi_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)}{\Phi_{\mathrm{N}}(r)}
  = 1 - \frac{4}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r} + \frac{1}{3}\,e^{-m_0 r},}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Psi-local}
  \boxed{
  \frac{\Psi_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)}{\Psi_{\mathrm{N}}(r)}
  = 1 - \frac{2}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r} - \frac{1}{3}\,e^{-m_0 r}.}
\end{equation}
At conformal coupling ($\xi = 1/6$, $m_0 = \infty$), the scalar terms
are absent:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\Phi_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)}{\Phi_{\mathrm{N}}(r)}\bigg|_{\xi=1/6}
  = 1 - \frac{4}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r},
  \qquad
  \frac{\Psi_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)}{\Psi_{\mathrm{N}}(r)}\bigg|_{\xi=1/6}
  = 1 - \frac{2}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Substitute $\KPhi^{\mathrm{loc}}$ and $\KPsi^{\mathrm{loc}}$ into
\eqref{eq:Phi-exact}--\eqref{eq:Psi-exact} and apply the identity
\eqref{eq:GR-identity} term by term.  Note that
$4/3 \cdot 1 - 1/3 \cdot 1 = 1$ and $2/3 \cdot 1 + 1/3 \cdot 1 = 1$,
confirming the GR limit at large $r$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The coefficients $4/3$ and $2/3$ for the spin-2 Yukawa, and $\pm 1/3$
for the scalar Yukawa, are fixed by the spin decomposition of the
graviton propagator.  They are universal for all higher-derivative gravity
theories with the Weyl$^2$ + $R^2$ structure, and agree with Stelle (1977)
and Edholm, Koshelev, Mazumdar (2016).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Short-Distance Structure}
\label{sec:short-distance}

\begin{proposition}[Short-distance limits]
\label{prop:short-distance}
For $\xi \neq 1/6$, the local potentials are regular at the origin:
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)
  = GM\left(\frac{4}{3}\,m_2 - \frac{1}{3}\,m_0\right) + \order(r),
  \qquad r \to 0.
\end{equation}
At exact conformal coupling,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)\big|_{\xi=1/6}
  \sim -\frac{GM}{3r},
  \qquad r \to 0.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Expand $e^{-mr} = 1 - mr + \order(r^2)$ in
\cref{eq:Phi-local,eq:Psi-local}.  The coefficient of $1/r$ is
$1 - 4/3 + 1/3 = 0$ when $\xi \neq 1/6$.  At conformal coupling, the
scalar term is absent and $1 - 4/3 = -1/3$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The conformal and short-distance limits do not commute: taking
$\xi \to 1/6$ at fixed $r > 0$ removes the scalar Yukawa tail, whereas
taking $r \to 0$ first at non-conformal $\xi$ shows the cancellation of
the Newtonian singularity.
\end{remark}

\section{The Linear PPN Parameter \texorpdfstring{$\gamma(r)$}{gamma(r)}}
\label{sec:gamma}

\subsection{Definition and Derivation}

The linear PPN parameter is defined as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-def}
  \gamma(r) \coloneqq \frac{\Psi(r)}{\Phi(r)}.
\end{equation}
In GR, $\gamma = 1$ identically.  Within the Level~2 approximation:

\begin{proposition}[Level~2 gamma]
\label{prop:gamma}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-local}
  \boxed{
  \gamma_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)
  =
  \frac{
    1 - \frac{2}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r} - \frac{1}{3}\,e^{-m_0 r}
  }{
    1 - \frac{4}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r} + \frac{1}{3}\,e^{-m_0 r}
  }.}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Large-Distance (GR) Limit}

\begin{proposition}[GR recovery]
For $r \gg 1/m_2$,
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_{\mathrm{loc}}(r) = 1 + \order(e^{-m_2 r}, e^{-m_0 r}).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Both exponentials vanish at large radius; numerator and denominator of
\cref{eq:gamma-local} both approach unity.
\end{proof}

More precisely, for $m_0 \geq m_2$ (which holds for all $\xi$ since
$m_0/m_2 = \sqrt{60/(78(\xi-1/6)^2)} \geq 1$ in the relevant parameter
range), the leading correction is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-asymp}
  \gamma(r) - 1 \approx \frac{2}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r}
  \qquad (r \gg 1/m_2).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Short-Distance Values}
\label{sec:gamma-short}

For $\xi \neq 1/6$, taking $r \to 0$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-zero}
  \gamma_{\mathrm{loc}}(0)
  = \frac{2m_2 + m_0}{4m_2 - m_0}.
\end{equation}
Numerical values for representative $\xi$:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
$\xi$ & $m_0/\Lambda$ & $m_2/\Lambda$ & $\gamma(0)$ \\
\midrule
$0$ & $\sqrt{6} \approx 2.449$ & $\sqrt{60/13} \approx 2.148$ & $\approx 1.098$ \\
$1/6$ (conformal) & $\infty$ & $2.148$ & $-1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
At exact conformal coupling, $\gamma(0) = (2/3)/(-1/3) = -1$,
reflecting the absence of the scalar mode that would otherwise
cancel the Newtonian singularity.

\section{Phenomenological Bounds on \texorpdfstring{$\Lambda$}{Lambda}}
\label{sec:bounds}
% =========================================================================

In this section, we extract lower bounds on the SCT scale $\Lambda$ from
five experiments.  The first three (Cassini, MESSENGER, Eot-Wash) constrain
$\gamma$ and yield direct bounds.  The last two (MICROSCOPE, LLR) constrain
the Nordtvedt parameter $\eta = 4\beta - \gamma - 3$ and require knowledge
of $\beta$, which is not yet derived.

\subsection{Cassini (Shapiro Time Delay)}
\label{sec:cassini}

The Cassini spacecraft measurement of the Shapiro time delay
(Bertotti, Iess, Tortora 2003) constrains
\begin{equation}
  |\gamma - 1| < 2.3 \times 10^{-5}
\end{equation}
at a distance $r \simeq 1\,\mathrm{AU}$.  Using the asymptotic form
\eqref{eq:gamma-asymp}:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{2}{3}\,e^{-m_2 r_\mathrm{AU}} < 2.3 \times 10^{-5},
  \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
  e^{-m_2 r_\mathrm{AU}} < 3.45 \times 10^{-5}.
\end{equation}
Taking logarithms:
\begin{equation}
  m_2\,r_\mathrm{AU} > -\ln(3.45 \times 10^{-5}) \approx 10.274.
\end{equation}
With $r_\mathrm{AU} \simeq 7.58 \times 10^{17}\,\mathrm{eV}^{-1}$ and
$m_2 = \Lambda\sqrt{60/13}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cassini-bound}
  \boxed{
  \Lambda \gtrsim 6.31 \times 10^{-18}\,\mathrm{eV}
  \qquad \text{(Cassini, Level~2 Yukawa, spin-2 dominant).}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{MESSENGER (Radioscience)}
\label{sec:messenger}

The MESSENGER radioscience analysis (Verma, Fienga, Laskar+ 2014)
provides a tighter constraint on $\gamma$:
\begin{equation}
  |\gamma - 1| < 2.0 \times 10^{-5}.
\end{equation}
The same analysis gives
\begin{equation}
  e^{-m_2 r_\mathrm{AU}} < 3.0 \times 10^{-5},
  \qquad
  m_2\,r_\mathrm{AU} > 10.41.
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:messenger-bound}
  \boxed{
  \Lambda \gtrsim 6.39 \times 10^{-18}\,\mathrm{eV}
  \qquad \text{(MESSENGER, Level~2 Yukawa, spin-2 dominant).}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Eot-Wash (Torsion-Balance Short-Range Test)}
\label{sec:eotwash}

Torsion-balance experiments (Adelberger, Gundlach, Heckel+ 2009) search
for Yukawa deviations from the Newtonian inverse-square law at sub-millimeter
scales.  The experimental constraint is parametrized as
\begin{equation}
  |\alpha|\,e^{-r/\lambda} < \text{bound}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the Yukawa coupling strength and $\lambda = 1/m$ is the
range.

In SCT, the spin-2 Yukawa term in $\Phi$ has coupling $\alpha_2 = -4/3$
(with $|\alpha_2| = 4/3 > 1$) and range $\lambda_2 = 1/m_2$.  The
Eot-Wash constraint $|\alpha| < 1$ at 95\% CL for
$\lambda \gtrsim 38.6\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ (Lee et~al.\ 2020, PRL~124, 101101)
means that the SCT Yukawa (with $|\alpha| = 4/3$) must have its range
\emph{below} the sensitivity threshold:
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{m_2} < 38.6\,\mu\mathrm{m}
  \simeq 1.96 \times 10^{2}\,\mathrm{eV}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Hence $m_2 > 1/(38.6\,\mu\mathrm{m}) \simeq 5.11 \times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{eV}$,
giving
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eotwash-bound}
  \boxed{
  \Lambda \gtrsim 2.38 \times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{eV}
  \qquad \text{(Eot-Wash, Level~2 Yukawa, range constraint).}}
\end{equation}
Since $|\alpha_\mathrm{SCT}| = 4/3 > 1$, using the $|\alpha| = 1$ exclusion
boundary at $38.6\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ is conservative; the actual bound at
$|\alpha| = 4/3$ may be slightly stronger (shorter excluded range).

This laboratory bound is fourteen orders of magnitude stronger than the
Cassini bound, because short-range tests probe distance scales where the
Yukawa corrections are large.

\begin{remark}[Hierarchy of bounds]
The ordering $\Lambda_\mathrm{Eot\text{-}Wash} \gg \Lambda_\mathrm{MESSENGER}
\sim \Lambda_\mathrm{Cassini}$ reflects the much shorter characteristic length
scale ($38.6\,\mu\mathrm{m}$ vs.\ $1\,\mathrm{AU}$) of the torsion-balance
measurement.  For a viable SCT scale, the Eot-Wash bound is the most
constraining.
\end{remark}

  \caption{PPN exclusion plot showing lower bounds on the SCT spectral

\subsection{MICROSCOPE (Weak Equivalence Principle)}
\label{sec:microscope}

MICROSCOPE (Touboul+ 2022) constrains violations of the weak equivalence
principle via the E\"otv\"os parameter:
\begin{equation}
  |\eta(\mathrm{Ti},\mathrm{Pt})| < 1.5 \times 10^{-15}.
\end{equation}
In the PPN framework, the Nordtvedt parameter is
$\eta_N = 4\beta - \gamma - 3$.  Since $\beta$ is not yet derived in
SCT (it requires the $\order(h^2)$ field equations from NT-4b), no
$\Lambda$ bound can be extracted from MICROSCOPE at this time.

If $\beta = 1$ (as in GR at leading order), then
$\eta_N = 4 - \gamma - 3 = 1 - \gamma$, which equals zero in the
$r\Lambda \gg 1$ regime.  The MICROSCOPE bound would then be
automatically satisfied.

\begin{equation}
  \Lambda \gtrsim \text{NOT DERIVED}
  \qquad \text{(MICROSCOPE --- requires $\beta$ from NT-4b).}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Lunar Laser Ranging (LLR)}
\label{sec:llr}

Williams, Turyshev, and Boggs (2004) use lunar laser ranging to constrain
the Nordtvedt effect:
\begin{equation}
  |\eta_N| < 4.4 \times 10^{-4}.
\end{equation}
As with MICROSCOPE, this bound requires $\beta$ and is not yet available:
\begin{equation}
  \Lambda \gtrsim \text{NOT DERIVED}
  \qquad \text{(LLR --- requires $\beta$ from NT-4b).}
\end{equation}

\section{Level 3: Nonlinear PPN Parameters}
\label{sec:level3}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{The Parameter \texorpdfstring{$\beta$}{beta}}

The nonlinear PPN parameter $\beta$ probes the $\order(h^2)$ structure of
$g_{00}$ and the nonlinear superposition principle of the field equations.
It requires solving the SCT field equations to second order in the metric
perturbation, which is the content of the NT-4b derivation.  No trustworthy
derivation of $\beta$ is available from the present (linear) analysis.

\subsection{Preferred-Frame Parameters
  \texorpdfstring{$\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3$}{alpha\_1, alpha\_2, alpha\_3}}

These parameters vanish in any metric theory that is derivable from a
diffeomorphism-invariant action with no prior geometry.  The SCT spectral
action is manifestly diffeomorphism-invariant (being constructed from
spectral invariants of the Dirac operator), so one expects
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = \alpha_3 = 0.
\end{equation}
However, a rigorous proof requires performing the full PPN reduction of the
nonlinear field equations, not merely invoking the symmetry.  The
off-shell gauge invariance verified in NT-4a provides indirect support
but is not a complete argument.  Accordingly, we mark the $\alpha_i$ as
\texttt{0 (diffeomorphism invariance)} with the caveat that formal
verification awaits NT-4b.

\subsection{Conservation-Law Parameters
  \texorpdfstring{$\zeta_1, \zeta_2, \zeta_3, \zeta_4$}{zeta\_1, ..., zeta\_4}}

In theories derived from a Lagrangian, local energy-momentum conservation
($\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$) follows from diffeomorphism invariance via
Noether's theorem.  This implies
\begin{equation}
  \zeta_1 = \zeta_2 = \zeta_3 = \zeta_4 = 0.
\end{equation}
The same caveat about formal verification applies.

\subsection{The Whitehead Parameter
  \texorpdfstring{$\xi_\mathrm{PPN}$}{xi\_PPN}}

The parameter $\xi_\mathrm{PPN}$ (not to be confused with the
non-minimal coupling $\xi$) measures preferred-location effects.
In Lagrangian-based metric theories it vanishes:
\begin{equation}
  \xi_\mathrm{PPN} = 0.
\end{equation}
Formal verification requires NT-4b.

\section{Summary and Outlook}
\label{sec:summary}
% =========================================================================

\subsection{What Is Established}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=2em]
  \item The exact linear static potentials are determined by the NT-4a
    propagator denominators,
    \cref{eq:Phi-exact,eq:Psi-exact,eq:KPhi,eq:KPsi} (\textbf{Level~1}).

  \item The local Yukawa approximation (\textbf{Level~2}) yields the
    analytic potentials \cref{eq:Phi-local,eq:Psi-local} with effective
    masses $m_2 = \Lambda\sqrt{60/13}$ and
    $m_0 = \Lambda/\!\sqrt{6(\xi-1/6)^2}$.

  \item The linear PPN parameter $\gamma(r)$ is given by
    \cref{eq:gamma-local}, with $\gamma \to 1$ at solar-system distances
    for any viable $\Lambda$.

  \item The current lower bounds on $\Lambda$ are:
    \begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{lrl}
    \toprule
    Experiment & $\Lambda_\mathrm{min}$ (eV) & Constraint type \\
    \midrule
    Cassini & $6.31 \times 10^{-18}$ & $|\gamma-1| < 2.3 \times 10^{-5}$ \\
    MESSENGER & $6.26 \times 10^{-18}$ & $|\gamma-1| < 2.5 \times 10^{-5}$ \\
    Eot-Wash & $2.38 \times 10^{-3}$ & $|\alpha| < 1$ at $\lambda = 38.6\,\mu$m \\
    MICROSCOPE & Not derived & Requires $\beta$ \\
    LLR & Not derived & Requires $\beta$ \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

  \item The nonlinear PPN parameter $\beta$ and the remaining eight
    parameters are \emph{not yet derived}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What Remains Open}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*., leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Level 1 numerics}: Proper evaluation of the
    Fourier--Bessel integrals for the exact nonlocal potentials,
    resolving the pole prescription issue
    (\cref{sec:prescription}).

  \item \textbf{$\beta$ from NT-4b}: The nonlinear PPN parameter requires
    solving the field equations at $\order(h^2)$.  This would unlock the
    MICROSCOPE and LLR bounds, and determine whether the Nordtvedt effect
    constrains $\Lambda$ more tightly than Eot-Wash.

  \item \textbf{Ghost interpretation}: The TT pole at $z_0 \approx 2.41$
    represents a massive spin-2 ghost with residue
    $R_2 \approx -0.49$ (50\% suppressed relative to Stelle).  The
    physical interpretation requires the full unitarity analysis from MR-2.

  \item \textbf{Running of $\Lambda$}: The bounds assume a fixed
    $\Lambda$.  In a Wilsonian picture, $\Lambda$ may run, modifying the
    effective Yukawa masses at different energy scales.
\end{enumerate}
